Neural Network-Based Axle Detection for Bridge Weigh-In-Motion Systems

Bachelor Thesis  ·  Summary  ·  May 2026

Problem

Bridge Weigh-In-Motion (B-WIM) systems estimate truck weights from bridge strain signals. The critical first step is axle detection — locating the exact sample positions where each axle crosses. This is a severe class-imbalance problem: only ≈0.35 % of samples are positive (axle events), giving a 287 : 1 negative-to-positive ratio. Existing rule-based methods struggle with noise and closely-spaced axles.

Dataset

  • 32,141 vehicle records, each a 1,300-sample strain signal
  • Ground truth: binary pulse array marking exact axle positions
  • 2–11 axles per vehicle (mean ≈ 4.5); 80 / 10 / 10 % train/val/test split

Methods Compared

  • Baseline — Savitzky-Golay filter + SciPy find_peaks (no ML)
  • CNN (U-Net) — 1D encoder/decoder with skip connections (~30 M parameters)
  • TCN — Temporal Convolutional Network with 9 dilated residual blocks (~2.7 M parameters; receptive field = 2,045 samples)

Key Design Choices

  • Loss: BCEWithLogitsLoss with pos_weight = 287 to handle class imbalance
  • Evaluation: axle-level matching with ±5 sample tolerance (greedy match)
  • Metric: F1, Precision, Recall, and MATE (Mean Absolute Timing Error in samples)

Key Findings

  • Both deep learning models achieve near-perfect detection on unseen vehicles
  • TCN outperforms CNN despite being 11× smaller (2.7 M vs 30 M params) — architecture matters more than model size
  • Both models converge fast: CNN in 5 epochs, TCN in 33 epochs
  • Axle timing error of ≈ 0.24 samples is operationally negligible for weight calculation

Limitation & Future Work

  • Train/test split is record-level, not day-level — may overestimate cross-temporal generalization
  • Future: day-level chronological splitting; test on unseen bridge or sensor configurations
  • Robustness experiment under synthetic noise and speed variation still pending
